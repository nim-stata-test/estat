Battery Degradation Analysis Report

Generated: 2026-01-17 | Data period: 2023-03 to 2025-12

1. Background

This analysis investigates whether a deep-discharge event affected the round-trip efficiency of the home battery storage system.

1.1 System Overview

The system consists of a solar PV installation with battery storage for self-consumption optimization. Battery efficiency is measured as the ratio of energy discharged to energy charged (round-trip efficiency), accounting for conversion losses in both directions.

1.2 The Deep-Discharge Event

Event Period: February - March 2025

Cause: Faulty inverter prevented battery charging

Duration: Approximately 8 weeks

Consequence: Battery remained in a deeply discharged state for an extended period

Deep discharge events are known to potentially cause irreversible capacity loss and efficiency degradation in lithium-ion batteries, particularly when the battery remains at low state-of-charge for extended periods.

1.3 Confounding Factor: Min SoC Setting Change

Important: After the deep-discharge event, the minimum state-of-charge (SoC) setting was changed:

  • Before event: Min SoC = 5% (usable capacity: 95%)
  • After event: Min SoC = 20% (usable capacity: 80%)

This change was made as a precaution to protect the battery from future deep discharges.

Why this matters: The min SoC change means the battery now operates in a different part of its capacity range (20-100% vs 5-100%). This could independently affect measured efficiency because:

  • Li-ion batteries may have different efficiency at different SoC levels
  • Shallower cycles (higher min SoC) might show different charge/discharge characteristics
  • The change makes it difficult to separate degradation effects from operational changes

Implication: Any observed efficiency change could be due to (a) actual battery degradation, (b) the min SoC setting change, or (c) both. These effects cannot be cleanly separated with the available data.

1.4 Research Question

Did the deep-discharge event in February-March 2025 significantly affect the battery's round-trip efficiency?

2. Methods

2.1 Data

  • Monthly battery charging and discharging totals from 2023-03 to 2025-12
  • Pre-event observations: 23 months
  • Post-event observations: 9 months
  • Event period (excluded from analysis): February-March 2025

2.2 Efficiency Metric

Monthly round-trip efficiency was calculated as:

Efficiency = Discharge (kWh) / Charge (kWh) × 100%

Months with less than 10 kWh of charging were excluded to avoid unreliable estimates.

2.3 Statistical Models

Model 1 (Basic):

Efficiency = β₀ + β₁(Time) + β₂(PostEvent) + ε

Model 2 (With Seasonal Controls):

Efficiency = β₀ + β₁(Time) + β₂(PostEvent) + β₃(Winter) + β₄(Spring) + β₅(Autumn) + ε

Where:

  • Time: Months since start of observation (captures natural degradation)
  • PostEvent: Binary indicator (1 if after March 2025, 0 otherwise)
  • Seasonal dummies: Winter, Spring, Autumn (Summer as reference)
  • β₂: The coefficient of interest - change in efficiency attributable to the event

Both models use heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (HC3).

2.4 Additional Tests

  • Welch's t-test: Compares mean efficiency between pre- and post-event periods
  • Matched-month paired t-test: Compares same calendar months before and after (e.g., Nov 2024 vs Nov 2025)

4. Conclusion

Statistically significant efficiency reduction observed after the deep-discharge event period.

  • Basic model: 10.8 pp reduction (p=0.0001)
  • Seasonal-adjusted model: 8.2 pp reduction (p=0.0024)
  • Matched-month comparison: 4.6 pp reduction (p=0.0001)

Efficiency changed from approximately 84% to 79%.

4.1 Important Caveat: Confounding

Causal interpretation is limited because two changes occurred simultaneously:

  1. The deep-discharge event (potential battery degradation)
  2. Min SoC setting change from 5% to 20% (operational change)

The observed efficiency reduction could be due to:

  • Degradation: Actual battery damage from prolonged deep discharge
  • Operating range: Different efficiency characteristics at higher SoC levels
  • Both factors combined

Without reverting the min SoC setting (which would risk further damage), these effects cannot be separated.

4.2 Recommendations

  • Monitor battery capacity and efficiency metrics going forward
  • The 20% min SoC setting is prudent protection against future deep discharge
  • Consider professional battery health assessment if efficiency continues to decline
  • Factor current efficiency (~79%) into energy management calculations
